Profitability inflection
Operating income nearly doubled from $6.95B in FY2023 to $10.4B in FY2024 — a 50% jump. Operating margin expanded 570bps to 26.7%. This reflects the payoff from three years of cost discipline: password-sharing crackdown driving net adds, ad-tier monetization ramping, and content spend leverage. Netflix guided for 28% operating margin in FY2025.
Password sharing crackdown
FY2022 was the crisis year — Netflix lost 1.2M subscribers in H1, its first subscriber decline in a decade. The response was the password-sharing crackdown launched in Q2 2023, which drove record net adds of +8.76M in Q2 2023 alone. By FY2024, total subscribers hit 301.6M — a 41.6M increase in a single year, the largest in company history.
ARPU complexity — FX & mix
Blended global ARPU masks a critical structural dynamic: UCAN ARPU ($17.29/mo) is 2.4x LATAM ($7.21/mo) and 2.1x APAC ($8.08/mo). As international markets (EMEA, LATAM, APAC) grow faster than UCAN, they dilute blended ARPU even when local-currency ARPU improves. This is why Netflix targets revenue growth over ARPU growth as its primary metric.
Advertising tier — the next growth lever
Netflix launched its ad-supported tier in November 2022 at $6.99/month. By mid-2024, the ad tier had ~40M monthly active users globally. The ad business is still early-stage — Netflix doesn't break out ad revenue — but management has guided for ad revenue to become "meaningful" by FY2026. This is the most significant new revenue stream since international expansion.
EMEA — largest but lowest ARPU
EMEA became the largest region by subscribers in FY2023 (88.8M) and grew to 99.7M in FY2024. But EMEA ARPU ($10.62/mo) has actually declined from $10.69 in FY2020 due to FX headwinds and competitive pricing in Eastern Europe. EMEA is a volume story, not a monetization story — pricing power in this region remains constrained.
FY2022 anomaly explained
Operating income dropped from $6.19B in FY2021 to $5.63B in FY2022 despite revenue growing $1.9B. Three factors: (1) subscriber losses in H1 2022 as post-COVID churn accelerated; (2) elevated content amortization from a back-weighted release slate; (3) FX headwinds on non-USD revenues. All three were transitory — the structural business was healthy throughout.
