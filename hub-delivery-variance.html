A weekly plan-vs-actual cost tracker for a 40-hub delivery network, decomposed into volume and rate effects using the standard FP&A bridge method — mirroring the weekly business review (WBR) workflow used by Amazon's Hub Delivery Finance team.

Data disclosure: Amazon does not publish Hub Delivery financials. This dataset is synthetic — generated to be realistic based on public information (the >100% YoY network growth cited in Amazon's own job postings, typical last-mile per-package economics, and standard FP&A plan structures). Every assumption is documented in the data generation script. This is a modeling exercise, not real Amazon data.
Stop 01 · The problem
Why did actual cost diverge from plan?

Hub Delivery Finance tracks weekly plan-vs-actual performance across a fast-growing network of small-business delivery partners. When actual cost misses plan, the first question is always the same: was it driven by volume (more or fewer packages than expected) or rate (paying more or less per package than budgeted)? This project builds that decomposition end to end — from raw weekly data, through SQL, to a WBR-ready chart and a written action plan.

Stop 04 · SQL
Bridge calculation, in query

The full decomposition runs as a single aggregation query against the weekly plan/actual table — no spreadsheet manipulation required.

-- Volume Effect = (Actual Vol − Plan Vol) × Plan Rate
-- Rate Effect = (Actual Rate − Plan Rate) × Plan Vol
SELECT week_num,
  SUM((actual_packages - plan_packages) * plan_rate_per_pkg) AS volume_effect,
  SUM((actual_rate_per_pkg - plan_rate_per_pkg) * plan_packages) AS rate_effect,
  SUM(actual_cost) - SUM(plan_cost) AS total_variance
FROM hub_delivery_weekly
GROUP BY week_num
ORDER BY week_num;

Six queries in total cover the plan/actual trend, the bridge, worst-variance hubs, churned-hub impact, regional rate drift, and new-hub ramp performance against plan.

Stop 05 · Output
Action plan, written for the business

Escalate to Ops for a per-hub rate card review in the regions with the steepest rate drift — five hubs in the Southwest and Midwest account for a disproportionate share of the unfavorable rate variance in the trailing 4 weeks.

Validate whether the volume shortfall reflects softer demand or a forecasting gap before lowering next cycle's plan targets — a favorable cost variance driven by under-delivery isn't necessarily good news.

Deep-dive the top 5 variance hubs individually to determine whether the driver is local labor market conditions, onboarding ramp delays, or a one-off operational disruption.
